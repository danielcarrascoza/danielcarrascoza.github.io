Overview

  • Built a predictive model on the RateBeer dataset (2,924,163 reviews, 110,299 beers, 29,265 users) to forecast overall beer ratings from taste scores and free-text reviews.
  • Explored whether ABV, taste sub-ratings, and review text sentiment could predict overall satisfaction — progressing from simple regression baselines to NLP-based classifiers.
  • 70/30 train-validation split for regression models; 90/10 split for text classification to manage the cost of vectorizing millions of reviews.

Dataset & Features

RateBeer Dataset

13 fields per review including beer name, brewer ID, ABV, beer style, and sub-ratings for appearance, aroma, palate, taste, and overall (scale 5–20). Null values cleaned; non-numeric characters removed before modeling.

Feature Selection

Primary regression feature: review/taste (0–10 scale, normalized). Target: review/overall. For text models, review text was vectorized via Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF. ABV explored as a standalone predictor but showed no clear linear trend with overall rating.

Key Findings

  • Taste sub-rating is a strong linear predictor of overall satisfaction — the regression models performed well despite their simplicity, reflecting the tight coupling of taste and overall scores in the RateBeer schema.
  • ABV alone showed no clear linear relationship with overall rating in scatter analysis, confirming that flavor perception — not alcohol content — drives user scores.
  • TF-IDF outperformed Bag-of-Words (23.52% vs. 21.13%) by weighting rare, discriminative terms and down-weighting high-frequency filler words in the review corpus.
  • Text-only classification underperforms taste-based regression, but demonstrates that raw review sentiment carries signal — a hybrid model combining taste features with TF-IDF vectors would likely outperform either approach alone.

Limitations & Next Steps

  • Text models were trained on a 10,000-entry subset due to compute constraints; training on the full 2.9M corpus would substantially improve accuracy.
  • The 196-class overall rating scale (0–20) makes pure text classification difficult — framing as a regression or coarser bucketing (low / mid / high) would be more tractable.
  • Incorporating collaborative filtering (matrix factorization) alongside content-based features would enable user-personalized recommendations beyond global rating prediction.
